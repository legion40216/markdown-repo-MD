Great question. What you're really asking is: how do I deconstruct a well-written explainer article so I can replicate its skeleton for any topic? Here's how to think about it.
Step 1: Strip it down to its bones
Ignore the content entirely and just look at the moves the writer makes:

Opens with a concrete, specific fact (the 8.805 GHz record)
Establishes the current "best case" baseline (IBM zEC12 at 5.5 GHz)
States the central claim/question clearly ("with current tech, 1 THz is impossible")
Lists the reasons why with subheadings
Ends with a forward-looking "what if" paragraph

That's your reusable skeleton. Hook → Baseline → Claim → Why → Future. You can drop any topic into this.
Step 2: Work backwards from the claim
Pick your topic, then ask: what's the most extreme version of this thing, and why can't we reach it yet? That tension — where we are vs. where we can't go — is the whole article.
Step 3: What to reference when you have little data
You don't need much if you source smartly:

Wikipedia — for baseline numbers (speeds, sizes, dates, records)
Britannica — more reliable for foundational explanations
HowStuffWorks — great for breaking down mechanisms plainly
Scientific American — accessible but credible for "future" sections
Guinness World Records — for the hook (records, extremes)

The trick is: one strong specific number beats three vague claims. The 8.805 GHz detail is what makes that article feel credible. Find your equivalent of that number first, and everything else builds around it.
Step 4: Fake the depth with structure
When you don't have much data, structure does the heavy lifting. Three subheadings with even one solid point each reads as authoritative. Readers perceive organized writing as more researched than it might actually be.

Here's the more general method:
The actual skill is asking "what job is this paragraph doing?"
Go through any article and label each paragraph with a function, not a topic. Things like:

"establishes stakes" / "why should I care"
"grounds the reader in reality"
"introduces the obstacle"
"gives evidence"
"pre-empts a counterargument"
"opens the door to uncertainty"

Once you label every paragraph by function, you'll see that the content is interchangeable but the functions repeat across almost every good explainer. You're basically extracting the underlying logic of persuasion, not the surface structure.

---

Then when you go to write your own piece:
You don't copy the sequence. You ask "what functions does my reader need, and in what order?" That'll naturally vary by topic.
For low-data situations specifically, the functions you almost always need are:

One thing that makes the reader feel the scale of the topic
One honest acknowledgment of what we don't know
One concrete anchor (a number, a name, an event)

Those three can hold up a short article on almost nothing.
The real reference for this kind of thinking isn't another article — it's looking up "rhetorical moves in academic writing" or "They Say / I Say" by Graff and Birkenstein. That book literally catalogs the functional moves writers make, which is exactly what you're after.

Right, so once you have your sources in front of you, the process is:
Read it, then close it.
Seriously. Read the article, close the tab, then write what you remember. What you naturally retain is usually the most important point anyway. What you write from memory will come out in your own words by default.
What you're actually extracting from any source is:

The fact or number (you just reference it, not copy it)
The concept behind it (which you re-explain in your own logic)
The implication (which is your own thinking anyway)

So from that processor article you'd take "8.805 GHz record" as a fact, understand that extreme cooling was needed as the concept, and then you say what that implies. That last part is where your voice comes in and where copying stops being an issue.
The practical habit is:
After reading a source, write one sentence summarizing what it told you. Not a quote. Just what you now know that you didn't before. Do that for each source, and by the time you start writing you're already working from your own notes, not someone else's sentences.
The deeper point is:
Your job isn't to report what sources say. It's to answer your reader's question using what sources gave you. That shift in mindset naturally moves you away from copying because you're focused on your reader, not the source.
Once you have a few of those one-sentence notes, you just arrange them in an order that answers the question cleanly. That's the article.

Search "thermal throttling" — that's the specific mechanism behind why heat kills clock speed

From the overclocking record:

Search "why overclocking has limits" — that's a natural follow-on question from the record you already noted


The method is: every term in your notes is a potential next search. You're not jumping to the answer, you're pulling on threads that are already in your hands.
That's how the research actually builds — each article gives you new vocabulary, and that vocabulary becomes your next search. You never need to search the conclusion, you search the terms and let them lead you there.
